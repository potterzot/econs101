Behavioral Economics

  • People are weird, and how can we incoporate that weirdness to better understand the choices people make and to use that in policy models?

Rationality

  • Rational choice - A choice made that leads to the most preferred outcome.

An economics fairy tale

Once upon a time, economists argued that people were rational because they implicitly calculated the value of all choices.

Along came a dragon

But people actually behave with systematic biases:

  1. They may choose a worse but more fair outcome
  2. They may be risk-averse when facing gains but risk-seeking when facing losses
  3. They use shortcuts to make choices rather than evaluating the full cost/benefit of each choice.

Even economists, who learn about this stuff in multiple ways, still exhibit “irrational” behaviors.

These are from Krugman and Wells, but its really even worse than this

Ways of being ‘irrational’

  • Misperceptions of opportunity costs (e.g.I stood in line for a free student lunch for over an hour)
  • Overconfidence (i.e.we think were better the less we know about something) ref: Dunning-Kruger effect
  • Unrealistic expectations about future behavior (why dont I get up in the morning at 6am?)
  • Mental accounting / counting dollars unequally
  • Loss aversion (the disutility of losing $100 is greater than the utility of gaining $100)
  • We find calculating choice difficult and sometimes unpleasant, so we avoid thinking carefully (Thinking: Fast and Slow)
  • We value something we own more than we value the same item if we dont own it
  • We are altruistic even when theres no possible benefit

And others An ongoing list is on wikipedia.

Misperceptions of opportunity cost

  • When we make choices, especially when we consider the opportunity cost of something free, we often undervalue or dont include the cost of our time in our decision.

Unrealistic expectations about future behavior

  • We attribute the best behaviors to our future self, while making poorer choices in the moment.

Other-regarding preferences (Ultimatum game)

You are paired with another student. I give you $10. You offer some amount to the other student. That student can accept, in which case you give them your offer and keep the rest, or reject, in which case neither of you gets anything.

Question 2 (Ultimatum game):

  • (Group A): You are paired with a student next to you. How much would you offer the other person? Do you think they would accept or reject?
  • (Group B): You are randomly paired with a student from a different class. How much would you offer the other person? Do you think they would accept or reject?

Does economic theory still apply to choice?

Krugman and Wells:

  • Rational models still provide good predictions in most markets. (If markets are perfectly competitive.)
  • Market forces may compel people to behave more rationally over time. (If people can observe outcomes quickly and if they want to learn and if the penalty is high enough to induce behavior.)
  • Assuming rationality makes modeling simpler. (At the potential cost of unrealist or incorrect conclusions.)
