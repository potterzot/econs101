How do we estimate demand?

  • Demand Schedule - A table of quantity demanded at different price levels.

  • Willingness to Pay - The maximum amount a person is willing to pay for a quantity of a good (e.g. eBay max).

A market model

A model of a market consists of:

  • Demand curve - A graphical representation of a demand schedule. A demand curve depicts how much quantity of a good would be demanded at different prices.
  • Supply curve - A graphical representation of a supply curve, showing how much quantity of a good is supplied at different prices.
  • Demand and supply curve shifts - A shift in demand or supply due to a change in income (for demand) or technology (for supply), or other factors.
  • Market equilibrium - The point at which demand and supply is equal (i.e. the curves intersect).
  • Changes in market equilibrium - How the market equilibrium changes in response to shifts in demand and supply.

Demand curves

Changes in demand

  • Movement along the demand curve - Due to a change in price, we move along the demand curve from the old price to the new price.
  • Shift in the demand curve - If demand shifts, then the entire curve moves to the left or the right.

Shifts in Demand

Shifts in demand can occur because of changes in:

  • Population - More people means more demand for goods, so the quantity demanded increases for any price point.
  • Income - If a population becomes more wealthy overall, the demand curve can also shift (though also likely pivots).
  • Tastes - Preferences can change in response to cultural shifts, major events, or fashion.
  • Expectations - Expectations about the future can affect demand (e.g. an incoming hurricane increases bottled water purchases).
  • Number of consumers - A change in the number of consumers, such as when the drinking age was raised to 21.

Substitutes and complements

  • Substitutes - Two goods are substitutes if a decrease in the price of one good decreases demand for the other good.

Examples include food away from home (FAFH) and food at home (FAH).

  • Complements - Two goods are complements if a decrease in the price of one good increases demand for the other good.

Examples include gas prices and milk.

Normal and inferior goods

  • Normal goods - A normal good is one in which an increase in income increases demand for that good.

Examples include pretty much all goods.

  • Inferior goods - Goods are inferior if an increase in income decreases demand for that good.
