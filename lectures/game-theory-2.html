The prisoners dilemma

The prisoners dilemma in payoffs

What makes a prisoners dilemma?

  1. One strategy leads to a good payoff for both players.
  2. But at that equilibrium, each player gets a higher payoff from switching.
  3. If both switch, the payoff to both players is lower.

Back to Ammeera and Dimitrios

The prisoners dilemma outcome

  • If Ameera plays Deny then its in Dimitrios best interest to play Accuse (since 0 > -2).
  • If Ameera plays Accuse then its in Dimitrios best interest to play Accuse (since -8 > -15).
  • The same is true in reverse. If Dimitrios plays Deny, Ameera should play Accuse, and if Dimitrios plays Accuse, Ameera should play Accuse.
  • The dominant strategy is for both players to accuse, receiving the largest penalty (-8,-8).

This is known as a Nash Equilibrium.

Nash Equilibrium

  • Nash Equilibrium - Is a set of strategies for each player in which no player can gain by switching to a different strategy, given the strategies of other players.

In the prisoners dilemma, (Accuse, Accuse) is the Nash Equilibrium.

Why the negative solution?

  1. Players did not place any value on the payoffs of the other person, and so did not internalize (take account of) the costs that their actions inflicted on the other.
  2. There is no way to make the player pay for harm caused to other players.
  3. They were not able to make an agreement beforehand about what each would do.

Solution 1: Other-regarding preferences

If players care about the outcomes of other players, then the payoffs change and the game may no longer be a prisoners dilemma.

This can happen when social norms are strong, which can be affected by technology.

A fourth solution: repeated games

  • Repeated Game - A game that players play multiple times with the same payoffs and strategies.
  • Tit-for-Tat - A strategy in which a player starts out cooperating, but therafter copies the other players previous action.
