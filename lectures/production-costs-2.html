Average Variable Cost

  • Average Variable Cost (AVC) - Total variable cost (TVC) divided by the total production. Variable costs consist of inputs that can be adjusted in the short term such as labor and supplies.

E.g. if a college track team produces 4 star athletes per year and they have variable costs of $2.5 million, the average variable cost is

\[ \frac{TVC}{Q} = \frac{2.5}{4} = 625,000 \; \mbox{dollars.} \]

Average Total Cost

  • Average Total Cost - Total cost divided by the total production. Total costs consist of the sum of total fixed costs and total variable costs.

E.g. if the college track team has fixed costs of $1 million and variable costs of $2.5 million, the average total cost is

\[ \frac{TC}{Q} = \frac{1 + 2.5}{4} = 875,000 \; \mbox{dollars.} \]

Marginal Cost

  • Marginal Cost - The additional cost of one more unit of output. Marginal cost is equal to the slop of the Total Cost Curve.

E.g. Suppose the college wants to produce 5 star athletes per year. They estimate that the total cost would be $1.1 million in fixed costs and $3 million in variable costs. Then the marginal cost of the 5th star athlete is

\[ \frac{\Delta TC}{\Delta Q} = \frac{(1.1 + 3) - (1 + 2.5)}{5 - 4} = 600,000 \; \mbox{dollars.} \]

The new Average Total Cost would be

\[ \frac{TC}{Q} = \frac{1.1 + 3}{5} = 820,000 \; \mbox{dollars.} \]

What does this imply about whether the college should increase their investment in star athletes?

Why is the ATC curve U-shaped?

  • Spreading Effect: On the one hand, each additional output lowers average fixed costs (AFC), so the ATC is declining,

  • Diminishing Marginal Returns: on the other, each additional unit faces diminishing marginal returns from labor, so the ATC is increasing.

At low levels of production, the cost savings due to the spreading effect are higher so ATC declines overall, but at higher levels of production diminishing marginal returns have a stronger effect so the ATC increases again.

The banana stand

  • Building the banana stand costs $100.
  • Bananas cost $0.49 per pound.
  • Cutting bananas has diminishing returns to scale (i.e. as we increase the amount of labor, the number of bananas each person can cut goes down – the marginal productivity of each additional worker is smaller than that of the worker before them.)
